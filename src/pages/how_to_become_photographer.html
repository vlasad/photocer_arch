How to become a photographer in 2022:
The Definitive Guide

Updated {{ page.date.toLocaleDateString("en-US", { year: 'numeric', month: 'long', day: 'numeric' }) }}

Every photographer had a unique beginning.

Some people began photographing before the advent of digital photography. Others took online programs, while others have full-fledged degrees that they proudly display in their offices.

Many people learn by getting back up and trying again after falling flat on their faces.

Step 1: Simply begin

The camera's various knobs and buttons can be intimidating, but the crucial thing is to simply take the initial step.

Look up terms like composition and exposure in the dictionary.

Make a list of what you like and don't like about each of your previous images (or photos from a photographer you love).

Begin by subscribing to a few photography blogs or enrolling in an online beginner's photography school.

Step 2: Take a lot of photos, then take more

Surprisingly, becoming a photographer requires the shooting of photographs.

But you don't have to wait till you understand the difference between shutter speed and aperture camera settings to start taking pictures.

You'll finally get out of auto mode. Shooting in automatic mode can help you practice a few things, such as timing and composition (which is what you leave in and what you leave out of the photo).

You'll only start to progress if you do a lot of trial and error, so get started.

Take note of what you like and don't like about these early photos, since this can assist guide you as you develop your talents.

Step 3: Become familiar with the camera you'll be using

How well do you know your camera?

A camera is only as good as the photographer behind it.

What is the most efficient method for changing the ISO? Is auto bracketing, double exposure, or time lapse available on your camera?

Investigate the features of your camera so that you know where to go when you need to access a setting quickly.

It's also a good idea to know your gear's limitations, in addition to knowing what and where the features are.

Is it possible to take a sharp photo with your lens set to a wide aperture?

Even with an entry-level camera, knowing the limitations of your equipment can help you shoot better photos.

Step 4: Get a solid understanding of the principles

Auto mode isn't all awful, but mastering the foundations that allow you to move out of auto mode and call your own pictures is essential if you want to truly become a photographer.

This requires an understanding of the three fundamentals of exposure: shutter speed, aperture, and ISO. Each option affects how light or dark your image is, as well as how much of it is in focus.

I'm embarrassed to admit that the fundamental concepts of aperture and depth of field took me a long time to understand when I first started out.

Learn the fundamentals of focus methods and composition in addition to exposure.

These are the elements that enable you to begin taking photographs in which you have complete creative freedom.

You don't have to take on all of them at once, but you must understand the fundamentals.

Step 5: Continue photographing.

Continue shooting and putting new abilities into practice as you learn new skills, whether through a class, a written guide, or a mentor.

Instead of memorizing the concept of aperture, set your camera to aperture priority mode and shoot the same image at several apertures.

Then analyze how those adjustments influenced the final image.

Learning in a class (or tutorial, book, or mentor) is excellent, but putting those knowledge into practice helps consolidate them and turn concepts into useful information.

Begin carrying a camera with you wherever you go, and begin photographing what inspires you.

The more you practice, the faster you'll go from amateur to professional photographer.

Step 6: Concentrate your efforts.

While experimenting in all directions is important, if you want to become a professional photographer, you'll need to focus on a specific genre of photography.

There are numerous options to select from, ranging from wedding photography to product photography.

Begin with something you enjoy.

Do you enjoy photographing portraits, travel, weddings, fashion, conceptual fine art photography, documentaries, or commercial work?

After you've narrowed your focus, focus on honing the precise talents required in that sub-category.

Photographers who specialize in portraits, for example, must master the technique of posing.

Wedding photographers must be able to photograph an outside ceremony in direct sunlight.

While specialization is important, don't limit yourself too much to something that won't provide you with enough cash to support a stable career.

Step 7: Make your photos perfect with post-production

Photo editing is frequently regarded as a form of art in and of itself.

Learn how to edit your photos after you've taken them - Photoshop is the most common picture editing program, but it takes time to master.

To learn how to improve your photos, start with the basics and work your way up to the advanced how-tos.

Step 8: Get some feedback

It's time to seek feedback now that you've mastered the fundamentals and even a few more advanced skills.

Every photographer learns and develops at a different rate in different areas, so having a skilled eye look at your photographs might help you figure out what you need to work on next.

Find someone that isn't scared to express themselves and is knowledgeable about photography.

Inquire with a mentor, join an online photography group, participate image contests, or seek comments on social media.

Don't be frustrated if there are a lot of defects - identifying what has to be fixed can be a difficult emotional exercise, but it's necessary for determining where to go next.

Simply recall where you began, write a list of things to work on, and keep shooting.

Step 9: Create a portfolio.

Those practice and feedback photographs are a good start, but you might need to expand your portfolio by staging a shoot or offering a discounted session to a friend.

Then, to make your images easier to find for potential clients, compile your best shots and find a home for them online through one of the many photographers' websites.

After that, work on increasing your visibility.

Share your work and promote your new business on social media.

Rather of attempting to master all of the social media sites at once, focus your efforts on one.

It is preferable to have a single robust platform than multiple weak ones.

Step 10: Never, ever, ever stop shooting

Even seasoned experts discover fresh ways to challenge themselves in the field of photography.

Continue to seek out methods to improve your skills, whether it's through learning new business skills or taking on unique photographic projects.

When photography becomes a career, the initial interest that launched the trip can fade a little - personal photography projects are a great way to rekindle that light.
